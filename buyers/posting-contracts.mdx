## What Makes a Good Contract

A well-written contract does three things:
1. Clearly describes what you need so providers can assess fit
2. Sets enforceable terms so there's no ambiguity if something goes wrong
3. Gives providers confidence that you're a serious, fair buyer

<Warning>
  The **Contract Standard** — the structured engagement terms — becomes the source of truth in any dispute. Vague deliverables and fuzzy acceptance criteria are unenforceable. Write them as if explaining to a third party with no context.
</Warning>

## Contract Fields

### Basic Information

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| **Title** | Short, descriptive headline (e.g. "React dashboard for SaaS app") |
| **Description** | Full scope of work, background context, and any special requirements |
| **Category** | Primary discipline — Tech, Design, Marketing, Legal, etc. |
| **Required Skills** | Specific skills you need (e.g. React, TypeScript, Figma) |
| **Location** | "Remote" or a specific place if in-person presence is needed |

### Pricing

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Hourly Rate">
    Set a **required hours** estimate and an **hourly rate** in JOOBs. Providers may counter-propose a different rate or number of hours.

    Best for: ongoing work, research, consulting, or anything where scope isn't fully defined.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Fixed Price">
    Set a **fixed total price** in JOOBs. Providers propose to deliver the full scope for that amount.

    Best for: well-defined deliverables with clear acceptance criteria.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

### The Contract Standard

The Contract Standard is the formal agreement embedded in your contract. It's what gets enforced. Fill it out carefully.

**Engagement Terms**
- `deadline` — The date by which all work must be delivered (required)
- `depositRequired` — Whether you require a deposit upfront
- `depositAmount` — Amount in JOOBs if deposit is required
- `latePenalty` — What happens if work is late (e.g. "5% per day after deadline") or `null`

**Scope of Work**
- `scopeSummary` — One or two sentences describing exactly what is being purchased
- `deliverables` — A list of **specific, verifiable outputs**. Not "a website" — instead: "A deployed Next.js app accessible at the agreed URL, with a working login flow and dashboard."
- `outOfScope` — Explicitly list what is NOT included. This prevents scope creep and protects both sides.

**Acceptance Criteria**
- `acceptanceCriteria` — A list of **pass/fail conditions**. Write each one as if explaining to a judge: "All listed features render correctly in Chrome and Firefox with no console errors."
- `reviewWindowDays` — How many days you'll have to review the work after it's marked complete (1–90 days; default is 7)

**Delivery**
- `deliveryMethod` — How work will be handed off: `file_upload`, `url`, `in_person`, `repository`, or `other`
- `acceptedFileTypes` — File formats you accept (e.g. `[".fig", ".pdf"]`) or `["any"]`

## Tips for Writing Deliverables

<Tip>
  Each deliverable should describe a **concrete artifact** that can be verified without subjective judgment.
</Tip>

**Weak (unenforceable):**
- "A well-designed website"
- "Marketing copy"
- "Code cleanup"

**Strong (enforceable):**
- "A deployed Shopify storefront at the agreed domain with the 6 product pages listed in this contract, each with working Add to Cart functionality"
- "Five 300-word blog posts in the provided brand voice guide, delivered as Google Docs with editing access"
- "Refactored checkout module with 100% test coverage on the payment flow, confirmed by running the existing test suite"

## After You Post

Once your contract is live:
- It appears in the Browse Contracts feed immediately
- Providers can view it and submit proposals
- You'll receive a notification (and DM) when a proposal arrives
- Contracts auto-expire after 7 days of inactivity if unfulfilled

<Note>
  You can delete a contract while it's still open (no accepted proposals). Once a proposal is accepted, the contract is locked.
</Note>
