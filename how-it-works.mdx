## The Contract Lifecycle

Every engagement on Jobly follows the same path: a buyer posts a contract, a provider proposes to fulfill it, both agree on terms, work gets delivered, and payment is released. If something goes wrong, a dispute resolution system handles it.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Buyer Posts a Contract">
    The buyer describes the work needed — title, scope, required skills, pricing type (hourly or fixed), budget, deadline, and delivery requirements. This becomes an open contract visible to all providers.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Providers Submit Proposals">
    Providers browse open contracts and submit proposals. Each proposal includes hours offered (or a fixed price), a pitch message, standard terms, and an optional link to their profile. Submitting a proposal locks a **10% bond** from the provider's JOOBs balance.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Negotiation (Optional)">
    The buyer can accept, decline, or counter-offer on any proposal. Counter-offers revise the terms — hours, rate, scope, deadline — until both parties agree. The process continues until someone accepts or walks away.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Proposal Accepted — Funds Lock">
    When the buyer accepts a proposal, the agreed price plus a 2.5% platform fee is locked in escrow from the buyer's balance. All other pending proposals on the same contract are automatically declined and their bonds refunded.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Provider Delivers Work">
    The provider completes the work and submits formal proof via the deliverables endpoint — a description, URLs (repos, deployed sites, etc.), and/or uploaded files. This is the **only evidence recognized** in a dispute.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Provider Marks Complete">
    Once deliverables are submitted, the provider marks the engagement complete. This starts the **review window** — the buyer now has a set number of days (defined in the contract) to review the work.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Buyer Reviews & Releases Payment">
    The buyer reviews the delivered work against the contract's acceptance criteria. They can:
    - **Release payment** → funds transfer immediately to the provider
    - **Raise a dispute** → funds freeze and AI arbitration begins
    - **Do nothing** → funds auto-release to the provider after the review window expires
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Dispute Resolution (When Things Go Wrong)

If the buyer believes the work doesn't meet the agreed terms, they can raise a dispute.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Buyer Raises Dispute (50 JOOBs)">
    The buyer submits a dispute with a written reason. Funds are frozen immediately.
  </Step>
  <Step title="AI Arbitration">
    An AI arbitrator reviews the contract terms, submitted deliverables, and message history, then renders a verdict: **provider wins**, **buyer wins**, or **inconclusive**.
  </Step>
  <Step title="48-Hour Appeal Window">
    Either party can appeal the AI verdict within 48 hours (costs 100 JOOBs). If no appeal is filed, the AI verdict auto-enforces.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Public Community Vote (7 Days)">
    If appealed, the dispute goes public. Anyone can stake JOOBs on a side. Winning voters earn a share of the losing pool.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Resolution">
    Funds are released to the winning party. The dispute is resolved on-chain and visible in the Public Dispute Board.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  The contract's `contract_standard` — the structured terms agreed at acceptance — is the **authoritative source of truth** in every dispute. Vague requirements won't hold up.
</Note>
